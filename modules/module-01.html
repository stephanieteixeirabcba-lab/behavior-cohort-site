Module 01 · For Meeting 1

Foundations: the neurodiversity paradigm & what affirming practice means

Before we meet, work through the readings and the recorded lecture below. The homework is a short reflection — not a test. Come to our first meeting with one question you'd like the group to discuss.

Overview

What this module covers

This module sets the foundation for everything that follows. You'll learn the core ideas of the neurodiversity paradigm and the social model of disability, read the critiques autistic self-advocates have made of traditional ABA, and understand the six commitments that define affirming practice in this course.

This module asks something of you. Some of what you read may challenge how you were trained, or ask you to reconsider things you have done in sessions. That is not comfortable. But the point of this course is not comfort — it is doing better work for the people we serve. Read with openness.

By the end of this module, you should be able to:

  • Explain the neurodiversity paradigm and the social model of disability in your own words.
  • Summarize the core critiques autistic self-advocates have raised about ABA.
  • Distinguish between "behavior that is harmful to the client" and "behavior that is simply autistic" — and describe why this distinction matters for treatment goals.
  • Identify the six commitments that ground our work in this course, and give one concrete example of each.

Reading the critiques

You will notice the ASAN paper and some autistic voices speak very differently about ABA than the peer-reviewed BAP articles do. That is intentional on our part. You need to hear both. If you feel defensive while reading, notice it — and keep reading.

Key distinctions for session work

Behavior we support change for — and behavior we don't

Not legitimate targets for reduction

  • Stimming that is not injurious (hand-flapping, rocking, vocalizing, spinning objects) — it regulates, communicates, and expresses.
  • Special interests — they are sources of joy, expertise, and connection.
  • Lack of eye contact — often uncomfortable or overwhelming for autistic people; not a social deficit to fix.
  • Preferring to be alone, scripting, echolalia (when serving communication or regulation).
  • "Quiet hands" and similar body-compliance targets — these suppress regulation and communication.

Legitimate areas for support, with client buy-in

  • Behaviors that cause the client genuine distress or injury (to self or others).
  • Skills the client or family has identified as meaningful to them (functional communication, daily living, safety skills).
  • Coping strategies the client wants to build.
  • Environmental adjustments that reduce distress.

If your current BIP targets anything in the first list

That is a supervision conversation to have — respectfully, with your supervising BA. Module 2 covers how to raise these concerns appropriately within your scope of practice. Do not unilaterally change a plan; do flag what you're seeing.
