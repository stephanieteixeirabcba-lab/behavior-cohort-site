Syllabus

Course overview & schedule

Forty hours of training in neurodiversity-affirming, trauma-informed, assent-based ABA practice — designed for paraprofessionals implementing behavior-analytic services under BCBA supervision.

Who this cohort is for

This cohort is designed for Registered Behavior Technicians (RBTs) and paraprofessionals implementing ABA under the supervision of a Board Certified Behavior Analyst (BCBA) or BCaBA. It is not a credentialing pathway and does not substitute for the BACB's required 40-hour RBT training, competency assessment, or ongoing supervision. It is a practice-deepening cohort grounded in current evidence and autistic-informed best practice.

Guiding commitments

Everything in this cohort is built on six non-negotiable commitments, drawn from the peer-reviewed literature and autistic-led scholarship:

  1. Autistic and neurodivergent clients are the experts on their own experience. We center their voices and preferences, including those of non-speaking clients.
  2. Stimming, special interests, and neurodivergent communication are not targets for reduction. They are regulatory, communicative, and part of the person.
  3. Masking is not a successful outcome. Visible neurotypical behavior achieved at the cost of distress, exhaustion, or identity is iatrogenic harm.
  4. Assent is ongoing and can be withdrawn. We read it continuously — not just at session start — and we honor withdrawal even from clients who communicate non-verbally.
  5. Aversives, planned ignoring of distress, and compliance-as-a-goal are not defensible. This is not a stylistic preference. The field has moved, and so will we.
  6. Trauma is assumed, not required. We design every interaction as if the client may have a trauma history, because often we do not know.

Learning objectives

By the end of the cohort, participants will be able to:

  • Articulate the core critiques autistic self-advocates have made of traditional ABA, and describe what affirming practice looks like in response.
  • Operate within the RBT scope of practice and recognize the limits of their role.
  • Read, interpret, and implement a behavior intervention plan (BIP) with fidelity — while flagging concerns to their supervising BCBA.
  • Use assent-based teaching strategies and recognize assent withdrawal, including from non-speaking clients.
  • Apply trauma-informed principles (safety, trust, choice, skill-building) in session.
  • Collect and report accurate data, including social-validity observations.
  • Support functional communication, including AAC, in clients who benefit from it.
  • Identify when to escalate concerns, when to pause, and when to stop.

Meeting structure

Each bi-weekly meeting runs two hours:

  • Check-in & homework review (20 min)
  • Topic discussion — questions from the module's readings and lectures (40 min)
  • Case consultation — one or two de-identified scenarios (50 min)
  • Wrap-up & homework preview (10 min)

A ground rule about what we discuss

All case material must be thoroughly de-identified before sharing — change names, ages (shift by several years), locations, and any other identifying details. If you are not the supervising BCBA, do not share details of a client's behavior plan outside of appropriate supervision channels. When in doubt, leave it out.

Completion

This cohort does not award letter grades. Completion requires:

  • Attendance at at least 8 of the 10 live meetings.
  • Submission of all homework assignments.
  • A final integrative reflection at Meeting 10.

Homework receives written feedback — not a grade. Your private progress page tracks submissions and feedback.

A note on evidence and sources

This cohort draws on peer-reviewed literature in behavior analysis and autism research, and on autistic-led scholarship and self-advocacy. Where these sources disagree, we present the disagreement honestly rather than resolving it quietly. A full reading list with citations lives on the Resources page.
